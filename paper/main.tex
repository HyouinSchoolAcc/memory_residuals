\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textbf{Memory Residuals}, a fixed-size jointly-trained
recurrent memory matrix $M_c \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ that an
\emph{otherwise frozen} pretrained LLM reads and writes through a
depth-routed cross-attention residual. The construction occupies a
previously empty corner of the long-horizon-memory design space:
$\mathcal{O}(1)$ storage in chain length, $\mathcal{O}(1)$ per-token
routing cost, and no backbone fine-tuning. Prior recurrent-memory
recipes (Block-Recurrent, RMT, AutoCompressors) hit the constant-cost
target but unfreeze the LM; retrieval and Memorising Transformers
keep the backbone frozen but pay $\mathcal{O}(T)$ in storage or
context. The primitive decomposes into four loosely-coupled
segments---a Perceiver-style writer, a per-token readout, a depth
router, and a single-scalar memory-channel floor---and the
segmentation is itself a contribution: each piece can be replaced
independently. Empirically, with $\sim$6\% trainable-parameter
overhead on a frozen Qwen3-0.6B and $\sim$1.5\,h of training on a
single H100, the primitive lowers callback cross-entropy on
LongMemEval-S by $+1.32 \pm 0.53$~nats (four seeds, 95\,\% bootstrap
CI $[+1.11, +1.55]$), preserves at 1.7B ($\Dseventeenmean$~nats,
$n{=}\NSEEDSseventeen$ seeds, every seed positive), and transfers
without retraining to two unseen callback corpora at 1.7B
($\sim$70\% of the in-domain gain). A matched control suite
(chain-shuffle, evidence-redaction, oracle-RAG) localises the gain
to \emph{chain-conditional context compression} rather than per-fact
recall, and three single-flag interventions
(\S\ref{sec:chainspec})---a per-category breakdown, a sparse-writer
ablation on a random-alphanumeric-code corpus ($+1.33$~nats with
positive chain-shuffle), and a contrastive auxiliary
($\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}{:} 0\!\to\!{+}0.010$ without disturbing the
headline)---resolve the ``isn't this just a learned chain prior?''
critique affirmatively. We treat the numbers as proof-of-life and
the segmentation as the durable contribution; the deployment target
is a life-long conversational agent on a frozen LLM whose memory
module is trainable in hours, not weeks, and swappable rather than
re-fine-tuned.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\paragraph{The asymptotic design space of long-horizon LLM memory.}
The operating cost of a long-horizon memory mechanism over a chain of
$T$ sessions breaks into three axes: \emph{storage} (state size as a
function of $T$), \emph{routing} (per-token inference cost paid to
consult that state), and whether the LM \emph{backbone is frozen}.
Existing approaches sit in distinct corners of this cube
(Table~\ref{tab:asymptotics}): long-context attention pays
$\mathcal{O}(T^2)$ routing; RAG keeps the backbone frozen but the
index is $\mathcal{O}(T)$ and per-query inference adds
$\mathcal{O}(kS)$ context tokens; the recurrent-memory family
(Transformer-XL~\cite{dai2019transformerxl},
RMT~\cite{bulatov2022recurrent},
Block-Recurrent~\cite{hutchins2022block},
AutoCompressors~\cite{chevalier2023adapting},
Compressive Transformer~\cite{rae2019compressive}) achieves
$\mathcal{O}(K)$ storage and routing but every prior recipe
unfreezes the LM during training. Memorising
Transformers~\cite{wu2022memorising} keep the backbone frozen but
pay $\mathcal{O}(T)$ in storage. \emph{No prior recipe sits in the
$\mathcal{O}(1)$/$\mathcal{O}(1)$/frozen-backbone corner with a memory
module that learns useful long-horizon behaviour.} Memory Residuals
is, to our knowledge, the first construction in that corner.

This corner is the only point in the design space simultaneously
cheap to deploy atop an existing pretrained LLM, cheap to run at
inference, and bounded in state regardless of conversation length.
A recipe that learns useful behaviour here is the natural target for
a life-long conversational agent on a fixed backbone, where
retraining the LM is prohibitive but constant per-conversation cost
is required.

\paragraph{What this paper contributes.}
(1) An $\mathcal{O}(1)$/$\mathcal{O}(1)$/frozen-backbone recurrent
memory primitive: a fixed-size jointly-trained matrix $M_c \in
\mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ ($K{=}128$) read and written through a
depth-routed cross-attention residual, trained end-to-end with
LM-NLL on chain-callback supervision; the LM backbone is never
updated. (2) \textbf{Composable segments} designed for follow-on
work: a Perceiver-style \emph{writer}
(\S\ref{sec:method_writer}), per-token \emph{readout}
(\S\ref{sec:method_read}), \emph{depth router}
(\S\ref{sec:method_router}), and a single-scalar \emph{$\alpha$-floor}
(the only auxiliary loss); interfaces are narrow and each piece can
be swapped independently. (3) A \textbf{chain-specificity
measurement framework}: matched controls (chain-shuffle
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$, evidence-redaction
$\mathrm{evidence\_lift}$, oracle-RAG) that separate
``chain-conditional context compression'' from ``literal evidence
retrieval''. (4) Empirical proof-of-life: $+1.32 \pm 0.53$~nats at
0.6B (four seeds, 95\,\% bootstrap CI $[+1.11, +1.55]$) and
$\Dseventeenmean$~nats at 1.7B ($n{=}\NSEEDSseventeen$ seeds, every
seed positive); transfer to two unseen callback corpora at 1.7B
recovers $\sim$70\% of the in-domain gain. The
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$ ``learned chain prior'' critique
yields to three single-flag interventions (\S\ref{sec:chainspec}):
a per-category breakdown surfaces hidden chain-conditional positives
in four of six LongMemEval categories; a sparse-writer ablation
produces $+1.33$~nats with positive chain-shuffle on a random-code
corpus; a contrastive auxiliary loss lifts chain-shuffle from
$\approx 0$ to $+0.010$ without disturbing the headline.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Long-context Transformers.} Recent benchmarks
(NoLiMa~\cite{modarressi2025nolima}, RULER~\cite{hsieh2024ruler},
Lost-in-the-Middle~\cite{liu2024lost}) show that even when the
context window is nominally long, effective use of distant positions
degrades sharply. We sidestep context extension entirely, keeping
the window at the 4\,k pretraining default and routing past-session
information through a fixed-size memory matrix.

\paragraph{Retrieval-augmented generation.} RAG~\cite{lewis2020retrieval}
indexes past sessions and appends retrieved sessions to the LM
context. The closest competitive simple-pipeline RAG with
long-context LMs is DOS-RAG~\cite{laitenberger2025dosrag}; our RAG
baselines mirror that recipe (per-session BM25 and MiniLM-L6 dense
embeddings, no re-ranking). An oracle cell gives the model gold
evidence-session indices directly, as a strict upper bound on
simple-pipeline RAG.

\paragraph{Recurrent / compressive memory in pretrained LLMs.}
Transformer-XL~\cite{dai2019transformerxl} and Compressive
Transformer~\cite{rae2019compressive} introduced segment-level and
compressed-tier recurrence. RMT~\cite{bulatov2022recurrent} and
Block-Recurrent Transformer~\cite{hutchins2022block} thread a
prepended memory token through segments. Memorising
Transformers~\cite{wu2022memorising} use kNN over past keys;
AutoCompressors~\cite{chevalier2023adapting} compresses prefixes into
soft tokens. All five live in the $\mathcal{O}(K)$/$\mathcal{O}(K)$
column of Table~\ref{tab:asymptotics} but unfreeze the LM during
training; Memorising Transformers keeps the backbone frozen at
inference but pays $\mathcal{O}(T)$ in storage. The
$\mathcal{O}(K)$/$\mathcal{O}(K)$/frozen-backbone combination is
missing. Two architectural choices put memres there: the memory is
read off the sequence axis through a depth-wise attention
residual~\cite{kimi2026attnres} (zero context-length cost), and the
backbone is frozen during training (any callback improvement must
flow through the memory module---leak-controlled by construction).

\paragraph{Attention residuals.} Our depth-router builds on Attention
Residuals~\cite{kimi2026attnres}, which replaces uniform depth-wise
addition with a softmax over intermediate sources. We register the
per-position memory readout $m^t$ as a foundational source in that
pool.

\paragraph{The RAG-vs-recurrent-memory comparison.}
LongMemEval~\cite{wu2024longmemeval} treats retrieval as the standard
baseline; we extend that comparison by using a \emph{frozen} backbone
in both arms, running an \emph{oracle} RAG cell with gold evidence
positions, and reporting chain-shuffle and evidence-redacted
controls for both methods.

\section{Memory Residuals}
\label{sec:method}

The primitive has three components: a recurrent \emph{writer} that
updates an internal state $M_c \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ per
session (\S\ref{sec:method_writer}); a per-token \emph{readout} that
produces a residual stream contribution $m^t \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times d}$
(\S\ref{sec:method_read}); and a \emph{depth router} that chooses
how strongly each layer reads from the memory pathway
(\S\ref{sec:method_router}). All three are jointly trained
end-to-end with LM-NLL on chain-callback supervision; the only
auxiliary loss is a soft floor on the depth-router's memory channel.

\subsection{Writer: two-stage QKV competition with iterative extraction}
\label{sec:method_writer}

Let $M_c^{t-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ be the persistent latent
state prior to session $t$ and $C_t \in \mathbb{R}^{N_t \times d}$
the token embeddings of the just-completed session. \textbf{Stage 1
(extraction).} A learnable query bank $M_{\mathrm{in}} \in
\mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ pulls salient features from $C_t$ via a
Perceiver-style~\cite{jaegle2021perceiver} $L_E$-step refinement:
$E^{(0)} = \mathrm{CrossAttn}(M_{\mathrm{in}}, C_t)$ and
$E^{(\ell)} = E^{(\ell-1)} + \mathrm{CrossAttn}(E^{(\ell-1)}, C_t)$
for $\ell \in \{1,\dots,L_E\}$ ($L_E{=}4$, $K{=}128$ throughout).
\textbf{Stage 2 (judge, slot-axis competition).} We concatenate
$P_{\mathrm{judge}} = [M_c^{t-1} \,\|\, E^{(L_E)}] \in
\mathbb{R}^{2K \times d}$ and apply a slot-attention~\cite{locatello2020slot}
update with QK-LayerNorm~\cite{zhai2023sigmareparam,ba2016layernorm}:
\begin{align}
A &= \underset{k \in [K]}{\mathrm{Softmax}}\!\left(
        \frac{\widehat{Q}\,\widehat{K}^{\top}}{\sqrt{d}}\right)
        \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times 2K},
& M_c^{t} &= \mathrm{LN}\!\left(M_c^{t-1} + \mathrm{GRUCell}(M_c^{t-1},
        A\,P_{\mathrm{judge}} W_V)\right). \label{eq:writer}
\end{align}
The slot-axis softmax forces the $K$ persistent slots to compete to
\emph{tile} the $2K$ candidates into disjoint specialist subsets,
removing the symmetric uniform fixed point a candidate-axis softmax
would admit.

\subsection{Readout: per-token cross-attention into \texorpdfstring{$M_c$}{Mc}}
\label{sec:method_read}

For session $X \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times d}$, the readout is a
cross-attention with token embeddings as queries and $M_c^{t}$ as
keys/values, refined through a $D{=}4$-step Perceiver-style stack to
produce $m^t \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times d}$. The readout depth is one
of two load-bearing design choices the ablation
(\S\ref{sec:abl}) identifies.

\subsection{Depth router with \texorpdfstring{$\alpha$}{alpha}-floor}
\label{sec:method_router}

We register $m^t$ as the foundational source $b_{-1}$ in the
attention-residual pool of \cite{kimi2026attnres}, alongside
per-block delta sources $b_0, \dots, b_{n-1}$. Each per-block
routing sublayer computes a softmax over the pool with a
\emph{memory-channel floor} auxiliary loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{floor}} = \lambda_{\mathrm{floor}} \cdot
  \max(0, \alpha_{\mathrm{floor}}^* - \mathbb{E}\,\alpha_{\mathrm{mem}})^2,
\label{eq:floor}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\mathrm{floor}} = 0.5$, target
$\alpha_{\mathrm{floor}}^* = 0.10$. The router is initialised so the
residual stream matches the bare backbone at step 0 (soft $\pm 4$
bias chosen for non-saturated softmax gradients from step 1).

\subsection{Frozen-backbone training and segment composability}
\label{sec:method_train}

The backbone---Qwen3-0.6B or Qwen3-1.7B \cite{qwen3}---is frozen
(\texttt{lr\_backbone}${=}0$). Only memres parameters train: 41.5\,M
($\sim$6\,\% overhead) at 0.6B and 164.8\,M ($\sim$8\,\% overhead) at
1.7B, for 1000 (0.6B) / 1500 (1.7B) TBPTT steps on the LongMemEval-S
450-chain training split. The objective is plain LM-NLL on callback
tokens plus Eq.~\ref{eq:floor}.

The four components above are deliberately \emph{loosely coupled}:
each is small, single-purpose, and replaceable without touching the
others. The segments interact through two narrow interfaces: $M_c
\in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ between writer and readout, and a
per-layer scalar mix $\alpha_l \in [0,1]$ between router and
backbone. Swapping the writer (e.g.\ for a state-space model) leaves
readout, router, and floor unchanged; swapping the depth-router for
a learned per-layer gate leaves $M_c$ unchanged. All four costs are
\emph{$T$-independent} (Table~\ref{tab:segments}), placing the
construction in the $\mathcal{O}(1)$/$\mathcal{O}(1)$ corner of
Table~\ref{tab:asymptotics}. Most prior recurrent-memory recipes
bundle writer, readout, and gate into a monolithic LM modification;
we ship the segmentation as a contribution so each piece has a
single testable job and follow-on work can replace it. The
ablations in \S\ref{sec:abl} verify each segment is individually
load-bearing.

\section{RAG Baselines and Eval Harness}
\label{sec:rag}

\paragraph{RAG cells.} We use a simple-pipeline RAG comparable to
DOS-RAG~\cite{laitenberger2025dosrag}, indexing each LongMemEval-S
session as a document. Four cells per backbone: \textsc{BM25 top-1}
(\texttt{rank\_bm25}), \textsc{BM25 top-3}, \textsc{dense top-3}
(\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}), and \textsc{oracle top-3} (gold
\texttt{chain\_evidence\_positions}). Retrieved sessions are
concatenated into the LM's context window and callback-token CE is
scored under the bare pretrained Qwen3 backbone. The oracle cell is
a strict upper bound on the simple pipeline.

\paragraph{Eval harness.} All numbers come from the same
callback-aware evaluator (\texttt{tools/eval\_callback.py}) on the
same 50-chain corpus (\texttt{lme\_val\_s512\_evpos.pt}). CE is
computed only over \emph{callback answer tokens} (1.4\,\% of total
chain tokens), avoiding the $\sim$70$\times$ dilution from
token-averaging. For every cell we report:
$\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}{=}\mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{nomem}}{-}\mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{method}}$
(larger is better);
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$ a chain-specificity confound (substitute
another chain's retrieved sessions or $M_c$); for memres only,
$\mathrm{evidence\_lift}{=}\mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{mem}}{-}\mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{mem,floor}}$
where the floor rebuilds $M_c$ with gold evidence sessions redacted
($\approx 0$ means the memory benefit is not literal evidence
retrieval); and per-chain positive count out of 50.

\section{Mechanistic Decomposition}
\label{sec:mech}

The two methods deliver \emph{different mechanisms}
(Figure~\ref{fig:mech}). \textbf{RAG is chain-specific but small}:
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \in [+0.23, +0.51]$ but $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}$
is only $+0.03$ to $+0.39$, since bare pretrained Qwen3 cannot
reliably exploit appended sessions for callback questions.
\textbf{MemRes is large but chain-shuffle-invariant}:
$\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}} \sim +0.98$--$+1.32$ (the readout is
\emph{trained} end-to-end), but $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$
because the writer encodes chain-conditional context that any other
chain's $M_c$ also carries (style, topic, prior turns)---\emph{not}
literal evidence retrieval. \textbf{Evidence-redaction confirms it}:
$\mathrm{evidence\_lift} = +0.001 \pm 0.006$ means $M_c$ built from
the redacted chain (gold evidence removed) is as effective as $M_c$
built from the full chain. RAG and memres are thus complementary:
RAG localises evidence; memres distils the chain into a context
vector.

\paragraph{``Isn't this just a learned chain prior?''}
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$ and $\mathrm{evidence\_lift}
\approx 0$ together suggest $M_c$ may be doing something close to a
chain-class prior. Two responses. (i) The relevant question is
matched-compute callback CE, and memres beats the best non-oracle
RAG cell by $+1.21$~nats while costing zero context tokens---not
trivially obtainable from a class prior alone. (ii) The
chain-conditional framing is what makes memres complementary to
RAG rather than redundant: a per-fact retriever and a chain-summary
compressor address orthogonal failure modes (lexical-mismatch
queries vs.\ context-budget exhaustion). We claim only that the
readout pathway carries $+1.21$~nats of chain-conditional signal the
bare backbone cannot extract from the raw token stream.
The plateau itself is a direct analogue of the early MoE failure
mode \cite{shazeer2017moe}, dissolved in that literature by
load-balance \cite{shazeer2017moe,fedus2022switch}, top-$k$
\cite{fedus2022switch}, and expert-choice routing
\cite{zhou2022expertchoice}; the writer-side toolbox for driving
specific information into a fixed memory is also well-developed
(extractive objectives \cite{wu2022memorising,zhong2022trime},
pointer/copy supervision \cite{vinyals2015pointer,see2017pointer},
slot-specialisation \cite{locatello2020slot}, key/value bindings
\cite{sukhbaatar2015memnets,miller2016keyvalue}).
\S\ref{sec:chainspec} runs three single-flag interventions in this
spirit; the plateau yields to all three.

\section{Lifting the chain-shuffle confound: three single-flag interventions}
\label{sec:chainspec}

\S\ref{sec:mech} predicted that the $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$
plateau is a training-objective artefact rather than a structural
property of the design corner. We test that with three
minimally-invasive interventions: (i) a per-category re-aggregation
of the same evaluation, no retraining; (ii) a single-flag
sparse-writer ablation on a no-class-prior corpus; (iii) a
single-flag contrastive auxiliary on the canonical recipe. All
three are sign-positive and corroborate each other.

\paragraph{(i) Per-category breakdown (no retraining).}
LongMemEval annotates each callback with one of six categories. We
re-evaluate the 1.7B checkpoints under a \emph{within-category}
chain-shuffle: for a chain whose callback sits in category $c$, the
shuffled comparison uses another chain's $M_c$ from the same
category. If $M_c$ encoded only category information, this
comparison would be benign. A positive ``hidden'' column
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh,samecat}} - \Delta_{\mathrm{sh,rand}}$ thus
measures chain-conditional content beyond the category prior. At
1.7B, four of six categories show a hidden positive of at least
$+0.02$~nats (Table~\ref{tab:percat}), cleanest in
\texttt{knowledge-update}---the category whose answers must come
from within-chain user-revised information.

\paragraph{(ii) Sparse-writer ablation on a no-prior corpus.}
The sharpest test of chain-specific binding is a corpus where any
class prior is provably unhelpful. \textbf{Synthd5} is a
random-alphanumeric-code callback corpus (``what is my
\textsc{ACCOUNT-ID} again?'') with a fresh five-character answer per
chain; the bare backbone has zero prior. A previous dense-writer
attempt collapsed: the writer fell into the symmetric uniform fixed
point of the slot-axis softmax, since plain LM-NLL has no
permutation-breaking pressure on high-entropy random targets
\cite{shazeer2017moe,locatello2020slot}. Adding a single flag
\texttt{--memres\_judge\_topk\_slot\_softmax 8} (top-$8$ slots per
input column, others masked) imposes expert-choice
routing~\cite{zhou2022expertchoice} at the architecture level. With
this flag (rest of recipe unchanged) training no longer collapses;
on $100$ held-out chains we get $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}} = +1.331$,
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} = +0.008$ (positive on a corpus where no class
prior can explain it), $\mathrm{evidence\_lift} = +0.002$. A
test-time fine-tuning probe ($M_c$-only optimisation against the
evidence session) returns $-0.078$~nats, indicating $M_c$-side
optimal storage is not yet recoverable through the trained
readout---the readout is the next bottleneck on this strict corpus.

\paragraph{(iii) Contrastive auxiliary on the canonical recipe.}
We add an InfoNCE-style contrastive auxiliary~\cite{oord2018cpc}
over $M_c$ representations (weight $0.5$, $200$-step warmup, unit
temperature) to the canonical recipe; it pulls together same-chain
representations and pushes apart different-chain ones, the simplest
permutation-breaking gradient channel without an architectural
change. The contrastive arm gives $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}} = +1.20$~nats
(within the headline CI) and lifts $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh,rand}}$ from
$-0.0005$ to $+0.010$. The categorical breakdown
(Table~\ref{tab:infonce_percat}) concentrates in the same
categories diagnostic (i) flagged: \texttt{knowledge-update}
$+0.012{\to}+0.031$, \texttt{single-session-assistant}
$+0.011{\to}+0.036$, both $\sim 3\times$.

\paragraph{Reading the three together.} The three differ only in

plateau is a training-objective property of the v1 recipe, not a
structural property of the design corner; it yields to the
specific-information levers the MoE analogy of \S\ref{sec:mech}
predicted. (ii) and (iii) are reported at $n{=}1$ seed (the
canonical headline took $n{=}4$ to lock a CI); we treat the
resulting recipes as the natural production candidates for
follow-on work, particularly when combined.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

\textbf{Single-corpus training; partial scale-dependent transfer.}
The writer is trained only on LongMemEval-S 450 chains. At 1.7B, two
of three callback-aware OOD corpora transfer cleanly (synthd4,
synthd4v2; every seed positive); synthd5 (random codes) does not, as
the in-domain $\mathrm{evidence\_lift} \approx 0$ predicts. At 0.6B
the small writer over-fits, motivating the in-domain headline at
0.6B and the cross-corpus claim at 1.7B. Full-chain-CE corpora
(LoCoMo, MSC) are small negatives at both scales; we make no
transfer claim there. \textbf{Bare-backbone RAG.} We compare against
a bare pretrained Qwen3 for RAG, so RAG cells lack an
instruction-tuned reading head; an instruction-tuned RAG backbone
would close some of the gap. \textbf{No unfrozen-backbone recurrent
baselines.} RMT~\cite{bulatov2022recurrent},
Block-Recurrent~\cite{hutchins2022block},
AutoCompressors~\cite{chevalier2023adapting} require fine-tuning the
LM. Running them on LongMemEval-S would mix ``memory module helps''
with ``LM capacity helps'' and we could not attribute movement in
$\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}$; a fair head-to-head requires freezing their
backbones (not engineered for) or unfreezing ours (which removes the
present primitive claim). \textbf{Chain-shuffle plateau partially
resolved, not locked at multi-seed.} \S\ref{sec:chainspec}'s three
interventions move $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$ in the predicted
direction; (ii) and (iii) are at $n{=}1$ seed, not the headline's
$n{=}4$. The full MoE-analogy toolbox (extractive supervision,
pointer/copy, slot-specialisation, key/value bindings) is
unexplored. \textbf{1.7B is $n{=}\NSEEDSseventeen$ seeds.} Every
1.7B seed lands positive (CI $\Dseventeenci$) but a confident
scaling claim past 1.7B would require more 1.7B seeds and a cell
at 4B+; out-of-scope here.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conc}

Memory Residuals is a recurrent memory primitive in a previously
empty corner of the (storage, routing, backbone) design space:
$\mathcal{O}(1)$ storage, $\mathcal{O}(1)$ routing, frozen LM
backbone. It decomposes into four small swappable
segments---writer, readout, depth router, and $\alpha$-floor---each
with a single testable job and a documented asymptotic cost; we
ship the segmentation as the durable contribution and the specific
instantiations as v1 implementations that follow-on work should
replace. The primitive lifts callback CE by $+1.32 \pm 0.53$~nats at
0.6B and $\Dseventeenmean$~nats at 1.7B, transfers to two unseen
callback corpora, and---once the right single-flag interventions
are added (\S\ref{sec:chainspec})---moves the chain-shuffle confound
in the direction the controls predict on a corpus where no class
prior could explain it. The deployment regime this construction
makes addressable---a constant-cost long-horizon memory module that
bolts onto a frozen off-the-shelf LLM, trains in hours rather than
weeks, and can be replaced or re-trained without touching the
backbone---is what we find most consequential. We claim only that
the corner is now demonstrably non-empty, that the segmentation
makes its successor easy to engineer, and that the chain-specificity
controls are sharp enough to tell when the next iteration has
actually moved.

\section{Bootstrap CI methodology}
\label{app:bootstrap}

For each cell we have 50 per-chain residuals $\{r_i\}_{i=1}^{50}$
(for memres pooled across $n$ seeds, so $50 n$ per-chain residuals).
We resample with replacement at the chain level $B = 5000$ times,
compute the mean of each resample, and report the $[2.5, 97.5]$
percentiles as the 95\,\% CI. This is the standard bootstrap of
\cite{efron1979bootstrap}; the cluster-by-chain choice respects the
fact that within-chain residuals are correlated (different methods
score the same callback positions).
