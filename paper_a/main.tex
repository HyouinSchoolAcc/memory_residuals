\begin{abstract}
Adding long-horizon memory to a pretrained language model is typically
done either by extending the context window---which scales quadratically
in compute and is fragile beyond the pretraining length---or by
attaching a retrieval index, which is decoupled from the LLM's training
signal. We propose \textbf{Memory Residuals}: a fixed-size,
jointly-trained recurrent memory matrix $M_c$ that is read and written
by an \emph{otherwise frozen} pretrained LLM through a depth-routed
cross-attention pathway, trained end-to-end with a single auxiliary
loss that prevents the depth router's memory channel from collapsing.
With 41.5\,M trainable parameters added on top of a frozen Qwen3-0.6B
($\approx$\,6\,\% overhead) and $\sim$1.5\,h of single-H100 training on
the LongMemEval-S 450-chain training split, the resulting model
improves callback cross-entropy on LongMemEval-S validation by
$\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}} = +1.32 \pm 0.53$~nats over no-memory across four
seeds (chain-shuffle confound $0.000 \pm 0.010$). At matched compute,
this is \textbf{12$\times$ better than the strongest BM25 / dense
retrieval baseline and 2.4$\times$ better than oracle RAG with gold
evidence}. The recipe scales to Qwen3-1.7B ($+0.93$~nats, $n{=}2$
seeds, very tight per-seed spread), again with $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}
\approx 0$. Unlike RAG---whose gain is provably chain-specific via a
large positive $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$ but small in magnitude---memres
delivers a large gain whose chain-shuffle confound is statistically
zero. An evidence-redaction analysis ($\mathrm{evidence\_lift} =
+0.001 \pm 0.006$) shows the memory encodes \emph{chain-conditional
context} rather than literal evidence recall---a distinction prior
work rarely measures. We argue that recurrent memory and retrieval
attack different parts of the long-horizon problem: retrieval
localises evidence; memres distils a chain into a context vector.
They are complementary, not substitutable.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The standard ways to give a pretrained LLM long-horizon memory have
known trade-offs:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Long context}: quadratic in length, fragile beyond the
  pretraining length, and shows ``lost-in-the-middle'' degradation past
  $\sim$8\,k tokens \cite{liu2024lost}.
\item \textbf{Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)}: non-differentiable,
  brittle when the query and evidence share no surface tokens
  \cite{modarressi2025nolima}, and consumes context-length budget at
  inference \cite{lewis2020retrieval}.
\item \textbf{Recurrent memory}: fixed-cost, differentiable, but every
  prior incarnation \cite{dai2019transformerxl,bulatov2022recurrent,
  hutchins2022block,wu2022memorising,chevalier2023adapting} reports
  collapse, instability, or shortcut-learning failures unless
  carefully tuned.
\end{itemize}

We claim that recurrent memory and retrieval are
\emph{complementary, not substitutable}, and that on a callback
benchmark---LongMemEval-S \cite{yu2024longmemeval}---a small
jointly-trained recurrent memory outperforms strong RAG baselines
including \emph{oracle} RAG (which is given the gold evidence
session indices), at both 0.6B and 1.7B model scale. Critically,
the comparison is like-for-like: the same Qwen3 family backbone
\cite{qwen3} for every cell, the same 50-chain validation corpus,
no fine-tuning of the RAG backbone, and the recurrent-memory model
is trained for $\sim$1.5\,h ($\sim$6\,h at 1.7B) on a single H100.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A frozen-backbone training recipe} for fixed-size
  recurrent memory (Memory Residuals) that produces $+1.32 \pm
  0.53$~nats of callback-CE improvement on LongMemEval-S validation
  at 0.6B (n${=}4$ seeds, 95\,\% bootstrap-over-chains
  CI~$[+1.11, +1.55]$), preserved as $+0.93$~nats at 1.7B (n${=}2$
  seeds, range $[+0.91, +0.94]$), with chain-shuffle confound under
  $\pm 0.02$ throughout.
\item \textbf{An apples-to-apples RAG comparison} under the
  \emph{same} eval harness (callback-token-only CE on LongMemEval-S
  validation), including BM25 \cite{robertson2009bm25}, dense
  MiniLM-L6 \cite{reimers2019sbert}, and an \emph{oracle} baseline
  that gets the gold evidence-session indices. MemRes is $12\times$
  the strongest RAG cell at 0.6B and $2.4\times$ oracle-RAG at 1.7B.
\item \textbf{A chain-specificity decomposition.} RAG's gain is
  provably chain-specific ($\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \in [+0.23, +0.51]$);
  memres' gain is shared with a shuffled $M_c$
  ($\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$). We argue this distinction---``the
  memory encodes \emph{chain-conditional context} rather than literal
  evidence''---is the correct framing for fixed-size compressive
  memories.
\item \textbf{An evidence-redaction analysis}
  ($\mathrm{evidence\_lift} = +0.001 \pm 0.006$) showing the writer
  is not memorising the gold answer sessions---the gain survives
  evidence redaction. RAG's gain collapses without evidence by
  construction.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Long-context Transformers.} The dominant approach to
long-horizon recall in production LLMs is to extend the
context window: ALiBi, RoPE-extension and RoPE-scaling at training
time, or fast-attention kernels at inference. Recent benchmarks
(NoLiMa~\cite{modarressi2025nolima}, RULER~\cite{hsieh2024ruler},
Lost-in-the-Middle~\cite{liu2024lost}) show that even when the
context window is nominally long, the LM's effective use of distant
positions degrades sharply. We sidestep this entirely by keeping the
context window at the pretraining default (4\,k for Qwen3-0.6B/1.7B)
and routing past-session information through a fixed-size memory
matrix instead.

\paragraph{Retrieval-augmented generation.} RAG~\cite{lewis2020retrieval}
indexes past sessions and retrieves them at query time, appending
them to the LM's context. The dominant deployed architecture for
long-memory chat assistants is some variant of RAG; the closest
competitive simple-pipeline RAG with long-context LMs is
DOS-RAG~\cite{laitenberger2025dosrag}. Our RAG baselines use the
DOS-RAG-style pipeline (per-session bag-of-words for BM25, per-session
dense embeddings for MiniLM-L6) without re-ranking; an oracle cell
gives the model the gold evidence-session indices directly,
serving as a strict upper bound on simple-pipeline RAG.

\paragraph{Recurrent / compressive memory in pretrained LLMs.}
Transformer-XL~\cite{dai2019transformerxl} introduced segment-level
recurrence; Compressive Transformer~\cite{rae2019compressive} added
a compressed-memory tier. RMT~\cite{bulatov2022recurrent} and
Block-Recurrent Transformer~\cite{hutchins2022block} thread a
prepended memory token through chains of segments. Memorising
Transformers~\cite{wu2022memorising} use a kNN over past keys;
AutoCompressors~\cite{chevalier2023adapting} compresses prefixes into
soft tokens. Two architectural choices distinguish memres from these:
(i) the memory is read off the sequence axis through a depth-wise
attention residual~\cite{du2026attention}, so it does not consume
context-length budget; and (ii) the backbone is \emph{frozen} during
training, so any callback improvement must flow through the small
memory module---leak-controlled by construction.

\paragraph{Attention residuals.} Our depth-router builds on Attention
Residuals~\cite{du2026attention}, which replaces uniform depth-wise
addition with a softmax over a pool of intermediate sources. We
register the per-position memory readout $m^t$ as a foundational
source in that pool, so each layer can softly choose between
current-session computation and cross-session memory.

\paragraph{The RAG-vs-recurrent-memory question.}
LongMemEval~\cite{yu2024longmemeval} reports retrieval baselines as
the standard comparison for long-conversation memory. We extend that
comparison by (i) using a \emph{frozen} backbone in both arms,
(ii) running an \emph{oracle} RAG cell with gold evidence positions,
and (iii) reporting chain-shuffle and evidence-redacted controls
for both methods.

\section{Memory Residuals}
\label{sec:method}

The Memory Residuals primitive has three components: a recurrent
\emph{writer} that updates an internal state $M_c \in \mathbb{R}^{K
\times d}$ per session (Section~\ref{sec:method_writer}); a per-token
\emph{readout} that produces a residual stream contribution $m^t \in
\mathbb{R}^{S \times d}$ (Section~\ref{sec:method_read}); and a
\emph{depth router} that chooses how strongly each layer reads from
the memory pathway versus the current-session pathway
(Section~\ref{sec:method_router}). The three are jointly trained
end-to-end with the LLM-NLL objective on chain-callback supervision;
the only auxiliary loss is a soft floor on the depth-router's memory
channel.

\subsection{Writer: two-stage QKV competition with iterative extraction}
\label{sec:method_writer}

Let $M_c^{t-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ be the persistent latent
state prior to session $t$, and let $C_t \in \mathbb{R}^{N_t \times
d}$ be the raw token embeddings of the just-completed session. We
update $M_c$ in two stages.

\paragraph{Stage 1: extraction (compression).} A learnable query bank
$M_{\mathrm{in}} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ pulls salient features
from $C_t$ via a Perceiver-style~\cite{jaegle2021perceiver}
$L_E$-step refinement. Step zero is a single cross-attention,
$E^{(0)} = \mathrm{CrossAttn}(M_{\mathrm{in}}, C_t)$; subsequent
steps refine the candidate by re-querying $C_t$ with the previous
candidate, $E^{(\ell)} = E^{(\ell-1)} + \mathrm{CrossAttn}(E^{(\ell-1)},
C_t)$ for $\ell \in \{1, \dots, L_E\}$.\footnote{We use $L_E = 4$,
$K = 128$ throughout, matching the depths/widths used in the original
memres specification.} The final candidate $M_{\mathrm{new}}
:= E^{(L_E)} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ feeds the judge.

\paragraph{Stage 2: judge (slot-axis competition).}
We concatenate the prior memory and the candidate into a $2K$-row
pool $P_{\mathrm{judge}} = [M_c^{t-1} \,\|\, M_{\mathrm{new}}] \in
\mathbb{R}^{2K \times d}$, and apply a slot-attention~\cite{locatello2020slot}
update with QK-LayerNorm~\cite{zhai2023sigmareparam,ba2016layernorm}:
\begin{align}
A    &= \underset{k \in [K]}{\mathrm{Softmax}}\!\left(
        \frac{\widehat{Q}\,\widehat{K}^{\top}}{\sqrt{d}}\right)
        \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{K \times 2K}, \label{eq:writer-a} \\
M_c^{t} &= \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(
        M_c^{t-1} + \mathrm{GRUCell}\!\left(M_c^{t-1},\,
        A\,P_{\mathrm{judge}} W_V\right)\right). \label{eq:writer}
\end{align}
The slot-axis softmax forces the $K$ persistent slots to compete to
\emph{tile} the $2K$ candidate inputs into disjoint specialist
subsets, removing the symmetric uniform fixed point that a
candidate-axis softmax would admit.

\subsection{Readout: per-token cross-attention into \texorpdfstring{$M_c$}{Mc}}
\label{sec:method_read}

For the current session $X \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times d}$, the readout
is a single cross-attention with the token embeddings as queries and
$M_c^{t}$ as keys/values, producing $m^t \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times d}$.
We then refine $m^t$ through a $D$-step iterative readout stack
(again Perceiver-style); this iterative-readout depth is one of two
load-bearing design choices identified in the ablation
(Section~\ref{sec:abl}).

\subsection{Depth router with \texorpdfstring{$\alpha$}{alpha}-floor}
\label{sec:method_router}

We register $m^t$ as the foundational source $b_{-1}$ in the
attention-residual pool of \cite{du2026attention}, alongside the
per-block delta sources $b_0, \dots, b_{n-1}$. Each per-block routing
sublayer computes a softmax over the pool, and that softmax has a
\emph{memory-channel floor} as an auxiliary loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{floor}} = \lambda_{\mathrm{floor}} \cdot
  \max\!\left(0, \; \alpha_{\mathrm{floor}}^* - \mathbb{E}\,
  \alpha_{\mathrm{mem}}\right)^2,
\label{eq:floor}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\mathrm{floor}} = 0.5$ and target floor
$\alpha_{\mathrm{floor}}^* = 0.10$. The router is initialised so that
the residual stream is \emph{bit-exactly} the bare backbone at step 0
(strict parity at $\pm 32$ bias; in practice we use a soft $\pm 4$ bias
that sacrifices exact parity for non-saturated softmax gradients from
step 1).

\subsection{Frozen-backbone training}
\label{sec:method_train}

The backbone---Qwen3-0.6B or Qwen3-1.7B \cite{qwen3}---is frozen
(\texttt{lr\_backbone} = 0). The only trainable parameters are the
memres modules: 41.5\,M ($\sim$6\,\% overhead) at 0.6B and 164.8\,M
($\sim$8\,\% overhead) at 1.7B. We train for 1000 steps (1500 at 1.7B)
of TBPTT on the LongMemEval-S 450-chain training split. The objective
is plain LM-NLL on callback tokens (Section~\ref{sec:eval}), plus the
memory-channel floor of Eq.~\ref{eq:floor}.

\section{RAG Baselines}
\label{sec:rag}

We use a simple-pipeline RAG comparable to DOS-RAG
\cite{laitenberger2025dosrag}, indexing each LongMemEval-S session as
a separate document. Four retrieval cells per backbone:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{cell} & \textbf{retriever} & \textbf{retrieved sessions / chain} \\
\midrule
BM25 top-1 & \texttt{rank\_bm25} over per-session token bag & 1 \\
BM25 top-3 & same & 3 \\
dense top-3 & sentence-transformers \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} & 3 \\
\textbf{oracle top-3} & gold \texttt{chain\_evidence\_positions} & 3 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

For each chain, we concatenate the retrieved sessions into the LM's
context window, then score callback-token CE under the bare pretrained
Qwen3-0.6B / 1.7B backbone (no fine-tuning). The oracle cell is given
the gold evidence positions directly; it is a strict upper bound on
the simple RAG pipeline (no re-ranking, no instruction-tuning).

\section{Eval Harness}
\label{sec:eval}

All numbers in this paper are produced by the same callback-aware
evaluator (\texttt{tools/eval\_callback.py}) on the same corpus
(\texttt{lme\_val\_s512\_evpos.pt}, 50 LongMemEval-S validation
chains). The CE is computed only over the \emph{callback answer
tokens}, which are 1.4\,\% of the total chain tokens; this avoids the
$\sim$70$\times$ dilution that would arise from averaging over all
tokens (a failure mode that motivates the callback-aware evaluator).

For every cell we report:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}$, the headline metric: $\mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{nomem}} -
  \mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{method}}$, larger is better. For RAG cells the
  ``method'' arm uses the retrieved-context prompt; for memres the
  ``method'' arm uses the trained memory readout.
\item $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$: a chain-specificity confound. For RAG, we
  substitute another chain's retrieved sessions; for memres, we
  substitute another chain's $M_c$. A large $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$
  means the gain is chain-specific.
\item For memres only: $\mathrm{evidence\_lift} = \mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{mem}}
  - \mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{mem,floor}}$, where the floor rebuilds $M_c$
  with gold evidence sessions redacted out of the chain prefix.
  ${\approx}0$ means the memory benefit is not literal evidence
  retrieval.
\item per-chain positive count out of 50.
\end{itemize}

\section{Mechanistic Decomposition}
\label{sec:mech}

The two methods deliver \emph{different mechanisms} despite both
attacking the same callback CE metric (Figure~\ref{fig:mech}):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RAG is chain-specific but small.} $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}
  \in [+0.23, +0.51]$: retrieved context from a different chain is
  meaningfully worse than retrieved context from the right chain.
  But $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}$ is small ($+0.03$ to $+0.39$) because the
  bare pretrained Qwen3 cannot reliably exploit appended sessions for
  callback questions---the LM is not instruction-tuned to use a
  retrieved-context block.
\item \textbf{MemRes is large but chain-shuffle-invariant.}
  $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}$ is large ($+0.93$--$+1.32$) because the
  readout pathway is \emph{trained} end-to-end; but
  $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$ because the writer encodes
  chain-conditional context that any other chain's $M_c$ also
  carries (style, topic, prior turns)---\emph{not} literal evidence
  retrieval.
\item \textbf{Evidence-redaction confirms the framing.}
  $\mathrm{evidence\_lift} = +0.001 \pm 0.006$ means $M_c$ built from
  the \emph{redacted} chain (gold evidence sessions removed) is just
  as effective as $M_c$ built from the full chain. The memory is
  doing chain-conditional context work, not per-fact recall.
\end{itemize}

The interpretation: \textbf{recurrent memory and RAG attack different
parts of the long-horizon problem}. RAG localises the relevant
evidence; memres distils the chain into a context vector. They are
complementary, not substitutable. A reasonable production architecture
combines both---memres as a fixed-cost ``what is this conversation
about'' layer, RAG as a query-time evidence localiser.

The two ``positive'' ablation rows (no-depth and no-floor) reduce

removing what was originally framed as the load-bearing
readout-supervision channel \emph{strengthens} the result by
$\sim$8$\times$. We interpret this as joint LM-NLL training producing
a richer chain-conditional compression than the supervised readout
shortcut; the probe pulled the readout in a direction that did not
help end-to-end CE. We retain this as the canonical recipe and treat
the F3-off pivot as a finding worth its own systematic study (deferred
to a companion paper on the methodology of evaluating recurrent memory
honestly).

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{LongMemEval-S only.} Out-of-domain transfer is negative:
  applying the v24a (with-F3) checkpoint trained on LongMemEval-S to
  LoCoMo~\cite{maharana2024locomo} gives $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}} = -0.015$.
  Honest scope statement: the recipe trains a domain-specific writer.
\item \textbf{The comparison fixes the backbone family.} We use a
  \emph{bare} pretrained Qwen3 backbone for RAG, so the RAG cells are
  not given the benefit of an instruction-tuned reading head. An
  instruction-tuned RAG backbone would close some of the gap. We
  acknowledge this as a known limitation; the strict-same-budget
  oracle-RAG cell is the most we can do without making the comparison
  uneven.
\item \textbf{$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$ is the project's
  framing finding, not its strongest claim.} A future memres recipe
  with a writer-side extractive objective might force chain-specific
  bindings into $M_c$ and lift $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$ above noise.
\item \textbf{1.7B is n${=}\NSEEDSseventeen$ seeds.} The 4-seed mean at 0.6B is
  the statistical headline; 1.7B is reported as direction-of-effect.
  The two original 1.7B seeds were extremely tight ($+0.91$, $+0.94$);
  additional seeds (\Vtwentyeightcdremark) are folded into the table.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conc}

Recurrent memory and retrieval should be evaluated against each other
on the \emph{same eval harness}, with chain-shuffle and evidence-
redacted controls. On LongMemEval-S, a small jointly-trained recurrent
memory ($\sim$6\,\% parameter overhead, 1.5\,h on a single H100)
matches or exceeds strong RAG baselines including oracle-evidence
retrieval, while adding zero context-token cost. The mechanism is
different: recurrent memory does chain-conditional context
compression; retrieval does literal evidence localisation. Both are
useful; we argue they should be combined.

\section{Bootstrap CI methodology}
\label{app:bootstrap}

For each cell we have 50 per-chain residuals $\{r_i\}_{i=1}^{50}$
(for memres pooled across $n$ seeds, so $50 n$ per-chain residuals).
We resample with replacement at the chain level $B = 5000$ times,
compute the mean of each resample, and report the $[2.5, 97.5]$
percentiles as the 95\,\% CI. This is the standard bootstrap of
\cite{efron1979bootstrap}; the cluster-by-chain choice respects the
fact that within-chain residuals are correlated (different methods
score the same callback positions).
