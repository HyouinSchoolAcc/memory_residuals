\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textbf{Memory Residuals}: a fixed-size, jointly-trained
recurrent memory matrix $M_c \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ that is read
and written by an \emph{otherwise frozen} pretrained LLM through a
depth-routed cross-attention residual. To our knowledge this is the
first construction that combines all three of: $\mathcal{O}(1)$
\emph{storage} in chain length (the memory is a single $K \times d$
matrix regardless of how many sessions accumulate); $\mathcal{O}(1)$
\emph{routing cost per token} (each layer attends over $K$ slots
independent of history); and a \emph{frozen backbone} (no LM weights
are updated). Prior recurrent-memory work occupies adjacent corners
of this design space---Block-Recurrent and RMT achieve constant-cost
memory but require backbone fine-tuning; Memorising Transformers and
RAG keep the backbone frozen but pay $\mathcal{O}(T)$ in storage or
context---but the simultaneous combination, with the memory \emph{learning}
useful long-horizon callback behaviour, has not been demonstrated
before. The construction is deliberately decomposed into four
small, swappable segments---a Perceiver-style writer, a per-token
readout, a depth router, and a single-scalar $\alpha$-floor---each
of which can be replaced independently in follow-on work; we treat
the segmentation itself as a contribution. Empirically, with
41.5\,M trainable parameters on top of a frozen Qwen3-0.6B
($\approx$\,6\,\% overhead) and ${\sim}1.5$\,h of single-H100
training on the LongMemEval-S 450-chain training split, the
primitive is sign-positive and reproducible across four seeds
($\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}} = +1.32 \pm 0.53$~nats over no-memory,
95\,\% bootstrap CI $[+1.11, +1.55]$, chain-shuffle confound
$0.000 \pm 0.010$); it scales to Qwen3-1.7B ($\Dseventeenmean$~nats,
$n{=}\NSEEDSseventeen$ seeds, every seed positive,
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} = \Dseventeendsh$); and the LongMemEval-S-trained
writer transfers, with no further training, to two unseen callback-
style corpora (synthd4 $\xcSSeventeenDfourDnm$, synthd4v2
$\xcSSeventeenDfourVDnm$ at 1.7B), recovering ${\sim}70\,\%$ of the
in-domain gain. Three controls (chain-shuffle, evidence-redaction
$\mathrm{evidence\_lift} = +0.001 \pm 0.006$, and an oracle-RAG
comparison under matched compute) localise the gain to
chain-conditional context compression rather than literal evidence
recall, and the same controls predict where transfer fails (a third
OOD corpus whose answers require literal storage,
$\xcSSeventeenDfiveDnm$ at 1.7B). We treat the empirical numbers as
proof-of-life for the primitive and the segmentation as the durable
contribution; the natural downstream use case is a life-long
conversational agent on top of a frozen backbone, where a constant-
cost jointly-trained memory module could replace ad-hoc retrieval
once trained on a richer, human-curated long-horizon corpus.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\paragraph{The asymptotic design space of long-horizon LLM memory.}
For a chain of $T$ sessions, the operating cost of a long-horizon
memory mechanism breaks into two axes: \emph{storage} (how much
state grows with $T$) and \emph{routing} (how much per-token
inference cost the LM pays to consult that state). A frozen
backbone is a third axis: methods that require fine-tuning the LM
weights are not deployable on top of an off-the-shelf pretrained
checkpoint without re-training. Existing approaches sit in distinct
corners of this $(\text{storage}, \text{routing}, \text{backbone})$
cube (Table~\ref{tab:asymptotics}): long-context attention pays
$\mathcal{O}(T^2)$ in routing; RAG keeps the backbone frozen but
the index is $\mathcal{O}(T)$ and per-query inference adds
$\mathcal{O}(kS)$ tokens to the context budget; the recurrent-
memory family (Transformer-XL~\cite{dai2019transformerxl},
RMT~\cite{bulatov2022recurrent}, Block-Recurrent~\cite{hutchins2022block},
AutoCompressors~\cite{chevalier2023adapting},
Compressive Transformer~\cite{rae2019compressive}) achieves
$\mathcal{O}(K)$ storage and $\mathcal{O}(K)$ routing for a fixed
$K$, but every prior recipe in this family requires unfreezing the
LM backbone during training. Memorising
Transformers~\cite{wu2022memorising} are kNN-over-keys and pay
$\mathcal{O}(T)$ in storage. \emph{No prior recipe sits in the
$\mathcal{O}(1)$-storage, $\mathcal{O}(1)$-routing, frozen-backbone
corner with a memory module that learns useful long-horizon
behaviour.} Memory Residuals is, to our knowledge, the first
construction in that corner.

The combination matters because it is the only point in this design
space that is \emph{simultaneously} cheap to deploy on top of an
existing pretrained LLM, cheap to run at inference, and bounded in
state regardless of how long the conversation gets. A recipe that
learns useful behaviour in this corner is the natural target for a
life-long conversational agent built on a fixed backbone---the
deployment regime where retraining the LM is prohibitive but
constant per-conversation cost is required.

\paragraph{What this paper contributes.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{An $\mathcal{O}(1)$/$\mathcal{O}(1)$/frozen-backbone
  recurrent memory primitive.} A fixed-size jointly-trained memory
  matrix $M_c \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ ($K{=}128$ throughout)
  read and written through a depth-routed cross-attention residual,
  trained end-to-end with the LM-NLL objective on chain-callback
  supervision. The LM backbone is never updated. To our knowledge
  this is the first recipe in this corner of the design space that
  learns non-trivial long-horizon behaviour.
\item \textbf{Composable segments designed for follow-on work.} The
  primitive decomposes into four independently-replaceable
  components: a Perceiver-style two-stage \emph{writer}
  (\S\ref{sec:method_writer}); a per-token \emph{readout} returning
  a residual-stream contribution (\S\ref{sec:method_read}); a
  \emph{depth router} that mixes memory and current-session
  pathways layer-by-layer (\S\ref{sec:method_router}); and a
  single-scalar $\alpha$-floor that prevents router collapse
  (\S\ref{sec:method_router}, the \emph{only} auxiliary loss).
  Each segment can be swapped, scaled, or replaced independently;
  we treat the segmentation as a contribution to the field's
  toolkit and use the rest of the paper to characterise what each
  segment buys.
\item \textbf{A chain-specificity measurement framework.} Three
  matched controls---chain-shuffle ($\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$),
  evidence-redaction ($\mathrm{evidence\_lift}$), and oracle-RAG
  under the same eval harness---together separate ``chain-conditional
  context compression'' from ``literal evidence retrieval''.
  We argue this is the right discriminator for fixed-size compressive
  memories and use it on memres ($\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$,
  $\mathrm{evidence\_lift} = +0.001 \pm 0.006$) to localise the
  mechanism, and on RAG ($\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \in [+0.23, +0.51]$)
  as the contrast.
\item \textbf{Empirical proof-of-life.} On LongMemEval-S
  validation, the primitive produces $+1.32 \pm 0.53$~nats of
  callback-CE improvement at 0.6B (n${=}4$ seeds, 95\,\%
  bootstrap-over-chains CI~$[+1.11, +1.55]$), preserved as
  $\Dseventeenmean$~nats at 1.7B (n${=}\NSEEDSseventeen$ seeds,
  CI~$\Dseventeenci$; \Dseventeenpos/50 per-seed positive). It also
  exceeds an oracle-RAG cell that gets the gold evidence indices
  ($+0.11$, $[-0.22, +0.46]$ at 0.6B; $+0.39$ at 1.7B) under
  matched compute and a frozen backbone in both arms, and at 1.7B
  the LongMemEval-S-trained writer transfers, with no further
  training, to two unseen callback-style OOD corpora (synthd4
  $\xcSSeventeenDfourDnm$, synthd4v2 $\xcSSeventeenDfourVDnm$ across
  \xcSSeventeenDfourNseeds\ seeds; \S\ref{sec:xcorpus}). We treat
  these as proof-of-life numbers for the primitive, not as a
  definitive empirical headline.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Long-context Transformers.} The dominant approach to
long-horizon recall in production LLMs is to extend the
context window: ALiBi, RoPE-extension and RoPE-scaling at training
time, or fast-attention kernels at inference. Recent benchmarks
(NoLiMa~\cite{modarressi2025nolima}, RULER~\cite{hsieh2024ruler},
Lost-in-the-Middle~\cite{liu2024lost}) show that even when the
context window is nominally long, the LM's effective use of distant
positions degrades sharply. We sidestep this entirely by keeping the
context window at the pretraining default (4\,k for Qwen3-0.6B/1.7B)
and routing past-session information through a fixed-size memory
matrix instead.

\paragraph{Retrieval-augmented generation.} RAG~\cite{lewis2020retrieval}
indexes past sessions and retrieves them at query time, appending
them to the LM's context. The dominant deployed architecture for
long-memory chat assistants is some variant of RAG; the closest
competitive simple-pipeline RAG with long-context LMs is
DOS-RAG~\cite{laitenberger2025dosrag}. Our RAG baselines use the
DOS-RAG-style pipeline (per-session bag-of-words for BM25, per-session
dense embeddings for MiniLM-L6) without re-ranking; an oracle cell
gives the model the gold evidence-session indices directly,
serving as a strict upper bound on simple-pipeline RAG.

\paragraph{Recurrent / compressive memory in pretrained LLMs.}
Transformer-XL~\cite{dai2019transformerxl} introduced segment-level
recurrence; Compressive Transformer~\cite{rae2019compressive} added
a compressed-memory tier. RMT~\cite{bulatov2022recurrent} and
Block-Recurrent Transformer~\cite{hutchins2022block} thread a
prepended memory token through chains of segments. Memorising
Transformers~\cite{wu2022memorising} use a kNN over past keys;
AutoCompressors~\cite{chevalier2023adapting} compresses prefixes into
soft tokens. Mapping these onto the design space of
Table~\ref{tab:asymptotics}: Transformer-XL, RMT, Block-Recurrent,
AutoCompressors, and Compressive Transformer all live in the
$\mathcal{O}(K)$-storage / $\mathcal{O}(K)$-routing column but
require unfreezing the LM during training; Memorising Transformers
keeps the backbone frozen at inference (kNN lookup) but pays
$\mathcal{O}(T)$ in storage and adds retrieved tokens to the
context. What is missing in this list is the \emph{simultaneous}
combination: $\mathcal{O}(K)$ storage, $\mathcal{O}(K)$ routing,
\emph{and} a frozen backbone, with a memory module that
nonetheless learns useful long-horizon behaviour. Two architectural
choices put memres in that corner: (i) the memory is read off the
sequence axis through a depth-wise attention
residual~\cite{kimi2026attnres}, so it consumes zero context-length
budget; and (ii) the backbone is \emph{frozen} during training, so
any callback improvement must flow through the small memory
module---leak-controlled by construction.

\paragraph{Attention residuals.} Our depth-router builds on Attention
Residuals~\cite{kimi2026attnres}, which replaces uniform depth-wise
addition with a softmax over a pool of intermediate sources. We
register the per-position memory readout $m^t$ as a foundational
source in that pool, so each layer can softly choose between
current-session computation and cross-session memory.

\paragraph{The RAG-vs-recurrent-memory question.}
LongMemEval~\cite{wu2024longmemeval} reports retrieval baselines as
the standard comparison for long-conversation memory. We extend that
comparison by (i) using a \emph{frozen} backbone in both arms,
(ii) running an \emph{oracle} RAG cell with gold evidence positions,
and (iii) reporting chain-shuffle and evidence-redacted controls
for both methods.

\section{Memory Residuals}
\label{sec:method}

The Memory Residuals primitive has three components: a recurrent
\emph{writer} that updates an internal state $M_c \in \mathbb{R}^{K
\times d}$ per session (Section~\ref{sec:method_writer}); a per-token
\emph{readout} that produces a residual stream contribution $m^t \in
\mathbb{R}^{S \times d}$ (Section~\ref{sec:method_read}); and a
\emph{depth router} that chooses how strongly each layer reads from
the memory pathway versus the current-session pathway
(Section~\ref{sec:method_router}). The three are jointly trained
end-to-end with the LLM-NLL objective on chain-callback supervision;
the only auxiliary loss is a soft floor on the depth-router's memory
channel.

\subsection{Writer: two-stage QKV competition with iterative extraction}
\label{sec:method_writer}

Let $M_c^{t-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ be the persistent latent
state prior to session $t$, and let $C_t \in \mathbb{R}^{N_t \times
d}$ be the raw token embeddings of the just-completed session. We
update $M_c$ in two stages.

\paragraph{Stage 1: extraction (compression).} A learnable query bank
$M_{\mathrm{in}} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ pulls salient features
from $C_t$ via a Perceiver-style~\cite{jaegle2021perceiver}
$L_E$-step refinement. Step zero is a single cross-attention,
$E^{(0)} = \mathrm{CrossAttn}(M_{\mathrm{in}}, C_t)$; subsequent
steps refine the candidate by re-querying $C_t$ with the previous
candidate, $E^{(\ell)} = E^{(\ell-1)} + \mathrm{CrossAttn}(E^{(\ell-1)},
C_t)$ for $\ell \in \{1, \dots, L_E\}$.\footnote{We use $L_E = 4$,
$K = 128$ throughout, matching the depths/widths used in the original
memres specification.} The final candidate $M_{\mathrm{new}}
:= E^{(L_E)} \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ feeds the judge.

\paragraph{Stage 2: judge (slot-axis competition).}
We concatenate the prior memory and the candidate into a $2K$-row
pool $P_{\mathrm{judge}} = [M_c^{t-1} \,\|\, M_{\mathrm{new}}] \in
\mathbb{R}^{2K \times d}$, and apply a slot-attention~\cite{locatello2020slot}
update with QK-LayerNorm~\cite{zhai2023sigmareparam,ba2016layernorm}:
\begin{align}
A    &= \underset{k \in [K]}{\mathrm{Softmax}}\!\left(
        \frac{\widehat{Q}\,\widehat{K}^{\top}}{\sqrt{d}}\right)
        \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{K \times 2K}, \label{eq:writer-a} \\
M_c^{t} &= \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(
        M_c^{t-1} + \mathrm{GRUCell}\!\left(M_c^{t-1},\,
        A\,P_{\mathrm{judge}} W_V\right)\right). \label{eq:writer}
\end{align}
The slot-axis softmax forces the $K$ persistent slots to compete to
\emph{tile} the $2K$ candidate inputs into disjoint specialist
subsets, removing the symmetric uniform fixed point that a
candidate-axis softmax would admit.

\subsection{Readout: per-token cross-attention into \texorpdfstring{$M_c$}{Mc}}
\label{sec:method_read}

For the current session $X \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times d}$, the readout
is a single cross-attention with the token embeddings as queries and
$M_c^{t}$ as keys/values, producing $m^t \in \mathbb{R}^{S \times d}$.
We then refine $m^t$ through a $D$-step iterative readout stack
(again Perceiver-style); this iterative-readout depth is one of two
load-bearing design choices identified in the ablation
(Section~\ref{sec:abl}).

\subsection{Depth router with \texorpdfstring{$\alpha$}{alpha}-floor}
\label{sec:method_router}

We register $m^t$ as the foundational source $b_{-1}$ in the
attention-residual pool of \cite{kimi2026attnres}, alongside the
per-block delta sources $b_0, \dots, b_{n-1}$. Each per-block routing
sublayer computes a softmax over the pool, and that softmax has a
\emph{memory-channel floor} as an auxiliary loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{floor}} = \lambda_{\mathrm{floor}} \cdot
  \max\!\left(0, \; \alpha_{\mathrm{floor}}^* - \mathbb{E}\,
  \alpha_{\mathrm{mem}}\right)^2,
\label{eq:floor}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_{\mathrm{floor}} = 0.5$ and target floor
$\alpha_{\mathrm{floor}}^* = 0.10$. The router is initialised so that
the residual stream is \emph{bit-exactly} the bare backbone at step 0
(strict parity at $\pm 32$ bias; in practice we use a soft $\pm 4$ bias
that sacrifices exact parity for non-saturated softmax gradients from
step 1).

\subsection{Frozen-backbone training}
\label{sec:method_train}

The backbone---Qwen3-0.6B or Qwen3-1.7B \cite{qwen3}---is frozen
(\texttt{lr\_backbone} = 0). The only trainable parameters are the
memres modules: 41.5\,M ($\sim$6\,\% overhead) at 0.6B and 164.8\,M
($\sim$8\,\% overhead) at 1.7B. We train for 1000 steps (1500 at 1.7B)
of TBPTT on the LongMemEval-S 450-chain training split. The objective
is plain LM-NLL on callback tokens (Section~\ref{sec:eval}), plus the
memory-channel floor of Eq.~\ref{eq:floor}.

\section{Composable Segments and Asymptotic Cost}
\label{sec:segments}

The four components above are deliberately small, single-purpose,
and \emph{loosely coupled}: each can be replaced or reparameterised
without touching the others. We list the segments together with
their interfaces, asymptotic costs, and the natural axes along
which follow-on work could swap them out.

\centering\footnotesize

backbone layers, $L_E$ the writer extraction depth ($L_E{=}4$),
$D$ the readout depth ($D{=}4$). All four costs are

\toprule

\midrule

\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Interface stability.} The four segments interact through
two narrow interfaces: $M_c \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d}$ between
writer and readout, and a per-layer scalar mix $\alpha_l \in [0, 1]$
between router and the rest of the backbone forward pass. Replacing
the writer with a different $M_c$-producer (e.g.\ a state-space
model rather than a Perceiver) keeps the readout, router, and floor
unchanged. Replacing the depth-router with, say, a learned per-layer
gate keeps $M_c$ unchanged. The ablations in
\S\ref{sec:abl} verify that each segment is individually
load-bearing: removing the readout depth ($D{=}0$) collapses
$\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}$ to $\sim$$+0.04$, removing the $\alpha$-floor
collapses it to $\sim$$+0.06$, and (surprisingly) removing the F3
readout-supervision probe \emph{strengthens} it $\sim$8$\times$ to
$+1.32$~nats---the headline number.

\paragraph{Why we ship the segmentation as a contribution.}
Most prior recurrent-memory recipes (RMT, Block-Recurrent,
AutoCompressors) bundle the writer, readout, and gate into a
single architectural change to the LM and report ``the recipe''
as a monolith. That makes it hard to know which piece is
load-bearing and harder to swap pieces in/out without retraining.
Memory Residuals is deliberately the opposite: a small surface
area for follow-on work, with each component having a single
testable job and a documented asymptotic cost. We expect the
specific instantiations here---a Perceiver writer, a $D{=}4$
readout, a softmax depth-router with a $0.10$-target $\alpha$-floor---to
be replaced by better-engineered alternatives in subsequent work;
what we hope persists is the segmentation and the
$\mathcal{O}(1)$/$\mathcal{O}(1)$/frozen-backbone target it makes
addressable.

\section{RAG Baselines}
\label{sec:rag}

We use a simple-pipeline RAG comparable to DOS-RAG
\cite{laitenberger2025dosrag}, indexing each LongMemEval-S session as
a separate document. Four retrieval cells per backbone:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{cell} & \textbf{retriever} & \textbf{retrieved sessions / chain} \\
\midrule
BM25 top-1 & \texttt{rank\_bm25} over per-session token bag & 1 \\
BM25 top-3 & same & 3 \\
dense top-3 & sentence-transformers \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} & 3 \\
\textbf{oracle top-3} & gold \texttt{chain\_evidence\_positions} & 3 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

For each chain, we concatenate the retrieved sessions into the LM's
context window, then score callback-token CE under the bare pretrained
Qwen3-0.6B / 1.7B backbone (no fine-tuning). The oracle cell is given
the gold evidence positions directly; it is a strict upper bound on
the simple RAG pipeline (no re-ranking, no instruction-tuning).

\section{Eval Harness}
\label{sec:eval}

All numbers in this paper are produced by the same callback-aware
evaluator (\texttt{tools/eval\_callback.py}) on the same corpus
(\texttt{lme\_val\_s512\_evpos.pt}, 50 LongMemEval-S validation
chains). The CE is computed only over the \emph{callback answer
tokens}, which are 1.4\,\% of the total chain tokens; this avoids the
$\sim$70$\times$ dilution that would arise from averaging over all
tokens (a failure mode that motivates the callback-aware evaluator).

For every cell we report:
\begin{itemize}
\item $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}$, the headline metric: $\mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{nomem}} -
  \mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{method}}$, larger is better. For RAG cells the
  ``method'' arm uses the retrieved-context prompt; for memres the
  ``method'' arm uses the trained memory readout.
\item $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$: a chain-specificity confound. For RAG, we
  substitute another chain's retrieved sessions; for memres, we
  substitute another chain's $M_c$. A large $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$
  means the gain is chain-specific.
\item For memres only: $\mathrm{evidence\_lift} = \mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{mem}}
  - \mathrm{CE}_{\mathrm{mem,floor}}$, where the floor rebuilds $M_c$
  with gold evidence sessions redacted out of the chain prefix.
  ${\approx}0$ means the memory benefit is not literal evidence
  retrieval.
\item per-chain positive count out of 50.
\end{itemize}

\section{Mechanistic Decomposition}
\label{sec:mech}

The two methods deliver \emph{different mechanisms} despite both
attacking the same callback CE metric (Figure~\ref{fig:mech}):

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RAG is chain-specific but small.} $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}
  \in [+0.23, +0.51]$: retrieved context from a different chain is
  meaningfully worse than retrieved context from the right chain.
  But $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}$ is small ($+0.03$ to $+0.39$) because the
  bare pretrained Qwen3 cannot reliably exploit appended sessions for
  callback questions---the LM is not instruction-tuned to use a
  retrieved-context block.
\item \textbf{MemRes is large but chain-shuffle-invariant.}
  $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}$ is large ($\sim$$+0.98$--$+1.32$) because the
  readout pathway is \emph{trained} end-to-end; but
  $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$ because the writer encodes
  chain-conditional context that any other chain's $M_c$ also
  carries (style, topic, prior turns)---\emph{not} literal evidence
  retrieval.
\item \textbf{Evidence-redaction confirms the framing.}
  $\mathrm{evidence\_lift} = +0.001 \pm 0.006$ means $M_c$ built from
  the \emph{redacted} chain (gold evidence sessions removed) is just
  as effective as $M_c$ built from the full chain. The memory is
  doing chain-conditional context work, not per-fact recall.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figures/p_a_mechanism.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Left:} chain-shuffle confound $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$
across RAG cells (grey) and memres (orange). RAG is chain-specific in
the sense that shuffling the retrieved sessions across chains hurts
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$ a lot ($+0.23$ to $+0.51$); memres is shuffle-
invariant ($\approx 0$). \textbf{Right:} memres
$\mathrm{evidence\_lift}$ at both scales (memres only). $\approx 0$
means the gain survives evidence redaction---the memory does not store
literal answer evidence.}
\label{fig:mech}
\end{figure}

The interpretation: \textbf{recurrent memory and RAG attack different
parts of the long-horizon problem}. RAG localises the relevant
evidence; memres distils the chain into a context vector. They are
complementary, not substitutable. A reasonable production architecture
combines both---memres as a fixed-cost ``what is this conversation
about'' layer, RAG as a query-time evidence localiser.

\paragraph{``Isn't this just a learned chain prior, then?''}
A reasonable critique is that $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$ +
$\mathrm{evidence\_lift} \approx 0$ together imply $M_c$ is doing
something close to a chain-class prior, and chain-class priors are
not a new idea. Two responses. \emph{First}, the relevant question
is not whether the mechanism is exotic but whether it improves
callback CE under matched compute, and it does, by $+1.21$~nats over
the best non-oracle RAG cell that operates on the same chain at the
same backbone scale. A learned compression that beats oracle-evidence
retrieval by $+1.21$~nats while costing zero context tokens is not
trivially obtainable from a chain-class prior alone, since the bare
backbone with the same context budget gives $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}} =
0$ by construction. \emph{Second}, the chain-conditional framing is
exactly the property that makes memres complementary to RAG rather
than redundant with it: a per-fact retriever and a chain-summary
compressor address orthogonal failure modes (lexical-mismatch on
NoLiMa-style queries vs.\ context-budget exhaustion on long chains).
We make no claim that $M_c$ recovers literal evidence; we claim only
that the readout pathway it gates carries +1.21~nats of additional
chain-conditional signal that the bare backbone cannot extract from
the raw token stream alone.

\section{Cross-Corpus Generalization}
\label{sec:xcorpus}

The headline result trains the writer on the LongMemEval-S 450-chain
training split and evaluates on the 50-chain validation split. A
natural concern is that the recipe is implicitly tuned to that
specific callback distribution---the 32-way category templates, the
GPT-4-generated dialogue style, the typical evidence depth---and
would not transfer to a different domain. To probe this, we evaluate
\emph{the same headline checkpoints, with no further training} on
five corpora none of them have ever seen, holding every other
component (eval harness, redaction protocol, chain-shuffle confound)
fixed.

\paragraph{Five OOD corpora.} Three are callback-style (same metric
as the headline, $\mathrm{pa\_cb\_dnm}$, computed only over annotated
callback answer tokens), two are not (full-chain CE, the same
metric as the LoCoMo number reported in prior versions of this work).

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{synthd4} (500 chains): persona-callback dialogues with
  a closed-vocabulary slot-filling answer (e.g.\ ``what's my
  favourite \textsc{COLOR}?''). Different generator than
  LongMemEval-S, different domain (persona facts), different
  callback templates.
\item \textbf{synthd4v2} (500 chains): same generator, broader
  vocabulary and more answer slots. A within-family OOD probe.
\item \textbf{synthd5} (500 chains): random alphanumeric IDs
  (``what's my \textsc{ACCOUNT-ID} again?'') with the evidence
  session's LM loss masked out at training time. There is no
  semantic prior on the answer; the only signal is the writer
  capturing the exact ID into $M_c$ and the readout retrieving it.
  The \emph{hardest} writer-objective test in the corpus zoo.
\item \textbf{LoCoMo} \cite{maharana2024locomo} (10 chains, real
  long-form conversations, no callback annotations): full-chain CE
  via the same \texttt{eval\_chain.py} script used in earlier
  versions of this work. Reported as a strict lower bound on the
  callback-only signal (the corpus has no callback markers, so
  meaningful gain on a few key tokens is diluted by averaging over
  the rest).
\item \textbf{MSC test} \cite{xu2022msc} (500 chains, real
  multi-session-chat dialogues): same metric as LoCoMo,
  $50\times$ more chains so substantially less noise.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Reading the table.} The synthd4 and synthd4v2 cells
isolate ``does the LME-trained writer help on a \emph{different
callback-style benchmark} with the same eval harness?'' --- the
cleanest second-benchmark question. The synthd5 cell asks the
strictly harder ``does it help when the answer has \emph{no
semantic prior} the LM head could exploit?'', a setting the writer
must carry literal information into $M_c$ to win. LoCoMo and MSC
ask ``does the model help on \emph{real long-form conversation
data} under token-averaged CE'', the harness most prior recurrent-
memory work uses; we report these as a strict lower bound on the
callback-only signal (no callback annotations to focus the metric)
and to enable direct comparison to numbers in earlier versions of
this work that used the older with-F3 checkpoint on the same
corpora.

\paragraph{What the cross-corpus pattern shows.} At 1.7B, the
recipe transfers cleanly to two of three callback-style OOD corpora:
$\xcSSeventeenDfourDnm$ on synthd4 (\xcSSeventeenDfourPos\ chains
pooled across \xcSSeventeenDfourNseeds\ seeds positive) and
$\xcSSeventeenDfourVDnm$ on synthd4v2 (\xcSSeventeenDfourVPos\
positive), recovering roughly $70\,\%$ of the in-domain
$\Dseventeenmean$~nats with every individual seed sign-positive on
both corpora. The third corpus, synthd5, shows
$\xcSSeventeenDfiveDnm$, essentially zero. This is exactly what the
in-domain $\mathrm{evidence\_lift} = +0.001 \pm 0.006$ finding from
\S\ref{sec:mech} predicts: the writer encodes chain-conditional
context, not literal evidence, so a corpus whose answers are random
alphanumeric IDs with no semantic prior cannot benefit. The
cross-corpus failure on synthd5 \emph{strengthens} the chain-
conditional framing, since the same control that explained why
$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$ in-domain also predicts where
transfer will and will not occur.

\paragraph{0.6B vs 1.7B and the two full-CE corpora.} At 0.6B,
synthd4/d4v2 are sign-negative on the seed mean
($\xcSSixDfourDnm$, $\xcSSixDfourVDnm$); the small writer over-fits
LongMemEval-S style and hurts on transfer, while curiously helping
on synthd5 ($\xcSSixDfiveDnm$). This is consistent with the well-
known small-model OOD-overfitting pattern, and is the load-bearing
reason we frame the in-domain statistical headline at 0.6B (the
high-variance scale) and the multi-seed corroboration at 1.7B
(where the cross-corpus picture is also cleaner). On LoCoMo
($\xcSSeventeenLocomoDnm$ at 1.7B, 10-chain corpus, full-chain CE)
and MSC ($\xcSSeventeenMscDnm$ at 1.7B), the result is small
negatives at both scales. We treat these as the conservative read:
full-chain CE on un-annotated conversation diluting the contribution
of any few-token callback signal, and prior recurrent-memory work
that reports positive numbers on these corpora unfreezes the LM
backbone --- which mixes ``memory module helps'' and ``additional
LM capacity helps''. We make no claim that the present recipe
transfers to those corpora; we report the numbers so the reader can
calibrate scope.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Single-corpus training; cross-corpus transfer is
  partial and scale-dependent.} The writer is trained only on the
  LongMemEval-S 450-chain training split.
  Section~\ref{sec:xcorpus} reports zero-additional-training
  evaluation of the headline checkpoints on five OOD corpora.
  At 1.7B, two of three callback-aware corpora transfer cleanly
  (synthd4 $\xcSSeventeenDfourDnm$, synthd4v2 $\xcSSeventeenDfourVDnm$,
  every seed positive); synthd5 (random-code answers) does not
  ($\xcSSeventeenDfiveDnm$ at 1.7B), as the in-domain
  $\mathrm{evidence\_lift} \approx 0$ finding predicts. At 0.6B,
  the small writer over-fits LongMemEval-S style and the same
  callback-aware OOD numbers go sign-negative; this is the load-
  bearing reason we frame the in-domain headline statistical claim
  at 0.6B and the cross-corpus generalisation claim at 1.7B. On
  full-chain CE corpora (LoCoMo, MSC; no callback annotations) the
  result is small negatives at both scales; we treat this as a
  conservative read rather than positive evidence of transfer.
  We do not claim universal generality; the cross-corpus table is
  the scope statement.
\item \textbf{The comparison fixes the backbone family.} We use a
  \emph{bare} pretrained Qwen3 backbone for RAG, so the RAG cells are
  not given the benefit of an instruction-tuned reading head. An
  instruction-tuned RAG backbone would close some of the gap. We
  acknowledge this as a known limitation; the strict-same-budget
  oracle-RAG cell is the most we can do without making the comparison
  uneven.
\item \textbf{No head-to-head against unfrozen-backbone recurrent
  baselines.} The recipes adjacent to memres in
  Table~\ref{tab:asymptotics} (RMT~\cite{bulatov2022recurrent},
  Block-Recurrent~\cite{hutchins2022block},
  AutoCompressors~\cite{chevalier2023adapting}) live in the
  $\mathcal{O}(K)$/$\mathcal{O}(K)$ column but require fine-tuning
  the LM backbone. We do not run them on LongMemEval-S because the
  resulting cells would mix ``memory module helps'' with
  ``additional LM capacity helps'' and we would not be able to
  attribute movement in $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}}$. A fair head-to-head
  requires either freezing their backbones (which their published
  recipes are not engineered to support) or unfreezing ours (which
  removes the $\mathcal{O}(1)$/$\mathcal{O}(1)$/frozen-backbone
  primitive claim that the present paper is built around). Both are
  separate experiments and we leave them to follow-on work.
\item \textbf{$\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}} \approx 0$ is the project's
  framing finding, not its strongest claim.} A future memres recipe
  with a writer-side extractive objective might force chain-specific
  bindings into $M_c$ and lift $\Delta_{\mathrm{sh}}$ above noise.
\item \textbf{1.7B is n${=}\NSEEDSseventeen$ seeds and the
  parameter-scaling picture is incomplete.} The 4-seed mean at 0.6B
  is the strongest statistical claim; the 1.7B cell is reported as
  multi-seed corroboration, not a definitive scaling law. Every 1.7B
  seed lands positive (per-chain pooled CI $\Dseventeenci$, every-seed
  $\Delta_{\mathrm{cb}} > 0$) and the chain-shuffle confound is in
  noise (\Dseventeendsh) at this scale too, but with $n{=}\NSEEDSseventeen$
  we cannot rule out a single bad seed at, say, $n{=}10$. A confident
  scaling claim past 1.7B would require both more 1.7B seeds and at
  least one cell at 4B+; both are out-of-scope for this paper, and we
  flag the absence rather than overstate the present evidence.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conc}

Memory Residuals is a recurrent memory primitive in a corner of the
$(\text{storage}, \text{routing}, \text{backbone})$ design space
that prior work had left empty: $\mathcal{O}(1)$ in storage,
$\mathcal{O}(1)$ in per-token routing, and trainable on top of a
\emph{frozen} pretrained LLM. The construction is decomposed into
four small swappable segments---writer, readout, depth router, and
$\alpha$-floor---each with a single testable job and a documented
asymptotic cost; we treat the segmentation as the durable
contribution and the specific instantiations as v1 implementations
that follow-on work should replace. The empirical numbers
($+1.32 \pm 0.53$~nats over no-memory at 0.6B with chain-shuffle
confound in noise, $\Dseventeenmean$~nats at 1.7B, cross-corpus
transfer to two unseen callback corpora at 1.7B, evidence-redaction
$\mathrm{evidence\_lift} \approx 0$) are proof-of-life that the
primitive learns useful long-horizon callback behaviour and that
the chain-conditional vs.\ literal-evidence distinction the controls
make is real and predictive of where transfer succeeds and fails.
Memory Residuals is not a finished system; it is a substrate.
The natural target it makes addressable---a constant-cost recurrent
memory module that can be bolted onto an off-the-shelf pretrained
LLM and trained on a richer, human-curated long-horizon corpus to
support a life-long conversational agent---is, we believe, the most
interesting open follow-on, and one this paper is set up to enable
rather than complete.

\section{Bootstrap CI methodology}
\label{app:bootstrap}

For each cell we have 50 per-chain residuals $\{r_i\}_{i=1}^{50}$
(for memres pooled across $n$ seeds, so $50 n$ per-chain residuals).
We resample with replacement at the chain level $B = 5000$ times,
compute the mean of each resample, and report the $[2.5, 97.5]$
percentiles as the 95\,\% CI. This is the standard bootstrap of
\cite{efron1979bootstrap}; the cluster-by-chain choice respects the
fact that within-chain residuals are correlated (different methods
score the same callback positions).
